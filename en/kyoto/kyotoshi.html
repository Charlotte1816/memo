Overview

Kyoto City was established in 794 as Heian-kyo and has long been the center of politics and culture in Japan. During the Heian period, the court flourished with aristocratic traditions, and numerous traditional arts and architectural styles were developed.

From the Kamakura period to the Muromachi period, although Kyoto experienced conflicts among feudal lords and warfare, it also saw cultural and artistic development with many temples and traditional festivals emerging. In the Edo period, the refined charm of Kyoto was re-appreciated, leading to a cultural revival.

Since the Meiji era, despite waves of modernization, Kyoto has preserved its history and traditions, and many historical sites and cultural assets continue to be passed down to the present. Today, the fusion of traditional landscapes with modern urban functions attracts visitors from both within Japan and abroad.

The city is characterized by its unique topography and natural environment. Located on a hillside stretching east and west, the northern part is surrounded by mountains with lush forests and steep slopes that highlight its natural beauty, while the central plain is interlaced by ancient waterways and canals that have shaped its historic townscape.

Arashiyama

Arashiyama is a scenic area in the western part of Kyoto City, long celebrated as a beautiful spot deeply rooted in Japanese aesthetics and cultural traditions. During the Heian period, it developed as a retreat for noble families who appreciated its seasonal splendor. The name “Arashiyama” is said to have been inspired either by the way the trees sway like a storm or by the rapid current of the Katsura River.

The iconic Togetsukyo Bridge spanning the Katsura River dates back to the Heian period when it was first constructed by a local governor and later expanded during the Ashikaga era. The bridge’s name is linked to Emperor Kameyama’s phrase likening the moon’s reflection on the river to a crossing, and its surroundings have long been a favored subject for poems, paintings, and Edo-period ukiyo-e prints.

Geographically, Arashiyama is bordered by the Katsura River to the east, Kogurayama to the west, and Atagosan to the north. With cherry blossoms in spring and vivid autumn leaves—often described as “Arashiyama’s brocade”—the area also features enchanting bamboo groves that add to its mystical charm.

Many legends surround Arashiyama. For instance, during the Sengoku period, several battles were fought along the banks of the Katsura River. At the foot of the mountains, it is said that Fujiwara no Teika—the compiler of the Hyakunin Isshu—established a hermitage known as “Shiguretei,” a site that remains a historic landmark.

Arashiyama also holds religious significance. Nearby temples such as Tenryu-ji were founded to honor deities and commemorate significant historical events. Tenryu-ji, built by Ashikaga Takauji to pray for the late Emperor Godaigo, features a strolling garden designed by Muso Soseki that has been recognized as a World Heritage Site.

Kurama

Kurama is located in the northern part of Kyoto’s Sakyo Ward, nestled between Mount Hiei and Kibune. Revered as a sacred mountain since ancient times, its name may derive from a legend of horse-riding ascetics or from the mountain’s saddle-like shape. Strategically placed in the “demon gate” region that guards the capital, its geography has long been celebrated for its spiritual power.

The famous Kurama-dera temple, founded in 770 during the Nara period, is central to the area’s mystique. According to legend, Fujiwara no Ise received a divine message from Bishamonten and established a hall dedicated to the deity here. It is also said that the young Minamoto no Yoshitsune (formerly known as Ushiwakamaru) learned the art of swordsmanship at Kurama-dera, raised by the mythical tengu. This legend is recounted in the narrative “Kurama Tengu” and has influenced literature and theater for generations.

Kurama centers around the approximately 580-meter-high Kurama Mountain, surrounded by deep cedar groves and winding valleys through which the Kurama River flows. In spring, the vibrant new leaves and in autumn, the colorful foliage make it a popular destination for hiking and nature walks. Scattered sacred sites such as the Gion-dō and Oku-no-in Maō-dō add to the area’s unique spiritual character.

Kurama is also famous for its annual fire festival held on October 22. In this dramatic event, participants carrying torches parade through the town—a tradition said to date back to the Heian period when the enshrinement of eight deities was meant to protect the capital.

Philosopher's Path

Philosopher's Path is a tranquil, approximately 2-kilometer walking trail in Kyoto’s Sakyo Ward. It runs from Ginkaku-ji to Nanzen-ji alongside the Biwako Canal, offering visitors a peaceful stroll amid cherry blossoms in spring, fresh greenery in early summer, vibrant autumn colors, and a serene winter landscape.

The name “Philosopher’s Path” originates from the late Meiji to early Showa periods when philosophers from Kyoto Imperial University—such as Kitaro Nishida and Tanabe Motonobu—would walk this route in contemplative thought. Nishida, in particular, cherished these reflective walks, and his commemorative stone inscribed with “I walk the path as I am” stands along the way as a silent testament to his legacy.

The area along this path has long been steeped in cultural refinement. Nearby are historic temples and shrines including Nanzen-ji, Eikan-do, Ginkaku-ji, and Hōnen-in, as well as several modest shrines and Jizo-dō. Additionally, the Biwako Canal—constructed during the Meiji period to bring water from Lake Biwa to Kyoto—enhances the scenic charm of the route.

Following post-war improvements, Philosopher’s Path became a beloved route not only for lovers of literature and art but also for tourists seeking to experience the reflective spirit that defines Kyoto.

Ohara

Ohara is a rural area located in the northeastern part of Kyoto’s Sakyo Ward. Nestled on the western foothills of Mount Hiei, it has been renowned since the Heian period as a peaceful retreat away from the city’s hustle and bustle. The name “Ohara” is thought to derive from vast open fields that once dominated the region, attracting those in search of solitude.

Ohara is rich in both religious and historical significance. It is home to temples such as Shorin-in of the Tendai school, Raige-in of the Jodo sect, and the famed nunnery Jakko-in. According to tradition, Jakko-in was founded by Prince Shōtoku and later became well known after Kenreimonin Tokuko, daughter of Taira no Kiyomori, spent her later years here in devotion after the downfall of the Taira clan—a story intertwined with the elegiac tone of the Heike Monogatari.

The natural beauty and history of Ohara have also been celebrated in the poetry and tales of the Heian period. A famous verse goes, “Ohara – even the mountains and fields recall the divine era,” capturing the deep impression that this tranquil countryside has made on generations. In later times, the term “Ohara Women” was coined to describe the local women who carried firewood on their heads down to Kyoto—a simple yet resilient image that has also been immortalized in art and literature.

Geographically, Ohara is a basin surrounded by the mountains of the Hiei range, lying at the source of the Takano River and blessed with abundant water and greenery. Traditional farmhouses still dot the landscape, and local products such as shibazuke pickles and handcrafted souvenirs keep the region’s cultural heritage alive.

In this way, Ohara is a place where nature, history, religion, and folklore blend harmoniously, creating an atmosphere of serene timelessness that continues to inspire visitors from all walks of life.

Mount Hiei

Mount Hiei is a sacred mountain straddling Kyoto and Shiga Prefectures, standing at 848 meters high. It has long been a center of Japanese Buddhism—especially for the Tendai sect—with deep historical and spiritual significance. Located to the northeast of the capital in the so-called “demon gate” region, the mountain has not only served as a guardian of Heian-kyo but is also considered a vast temple complex, commonly known as Enryaku-ji, founded in 788 by Saicho (Dengyo Daishi).

Saicho established a training hall on Mount Hiei to overcome the conservative nature of Nara Buddhism and to realize the Tendai ideal of “universal harmony.” Enryaku-ji later became known as the cradle of Japanese Buddhism, where prominent monks such as Hōnen, Shinran, Nichiren, Eisai, and Dogen studied and trained before founding their respective schools.

Geographically, Mount Hiei overlooks Lake Biwa to the east and the Kyoto Basin to the west, offering breathtaking panoramic views. Its natural environment—with ancient cedar and cypress forests, abundant wildlife, and mountainous flora—provides a fitting backdrop for ascetic training. Early mornings on the mountain, often enveloped in mist, create an atmosphere of solemnity that draws visitors into a reflective state.

Although Mount Hiei experienced turbulent times—such as the destruction wrought by Oda Nobunaga’s attack in 1571—the temple was later rebuilt and continues to welcome pilgrims and practitioners. The mountain embodies Saicho’s idea of “lighting up one corner of the world,” and its cultural significance, now recognized as a World Heritage Site, remains immensely valued.

Many legends also surround Mount Hiei. It is said that Saicho chose this mountain because it personified the Tendai ideal of “harmonious integration,” and the rigorous ascetic practices (such as the “sen-nichi kaihōgyō”) undertaken by its monks attest to its austere spirituality. Overlooking the capital, Mount Hiei continues to serve as a spiritual pillar for the people of Kyoto, representing a unique convergence of religion, history, and nature.

Kyoto Imperial Park

Kyoto Imperial Park is a vast green area located in the heart of Kyoto’s Kamigyo Ward, once home to the imperial palace and the aristocratic mansions of Heian-kyo. At its center stands the Kyoto Imperial Palace, and over the centuries, the grounds have served as a residence and ceremonial site for the emperor and his family. Covering approximately 65 hectares and stretching about 1.3 kilometers north to south and 0.7 kilometers east to west, it is now cherished as a tranquil haven for both locals and tourists.

Dating back to the Heian period with the establishment of the imperial palace, the park grew alongside Kyoto’s development with the formation of aristocratic neighborhoods. During the Edo period the area was arranged in a dignified manner around the palace, reinforcing the refined image of the imperial court. Following Emperor Meiji’s visit to Tokyo in 1869 and the subsequent relocation of the capital, the palace and its surrounding grounds gradually transformed into a public park.

Geographically, Kyoto Imperial Park is enclosed by earthen walls and hedges. Inside, visitors find expansive lawns, groves, ponds, and gardens. Centuries-old trees—including zelkova, camphor, cherry, and maple—display striking seasonal changes: cherry blossoms in spring, vibrant autumn colors, refreshing coolness in summer, and serene snowy sceneries in winter. Features such as small streams and the Shuisuitei garden epitomize the park’s tranquil beauty.

Historically, Kyoto Imperial Park has been the site of significant events. For example, during the Hamaguri Gate Incident (also known as the Forbidden Gate Rebellion), fierce clashes took place near the Hamaguri Gate over issues of loyalty and national ideals. Within the palace, structures such as the Shishinden and Seiryoden stand as enduring symbols of the imperial court’s refined culture.

Today, Kyoto Imperial Park—where nature and history converge in the very heart of the city—is open to the public as a national park, serving as a venue for leisurely strolls, educational visits, and tourism, while quietly upholding the enduring spirit of ancient Kyoto.
